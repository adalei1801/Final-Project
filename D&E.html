What Are The Small Things Are

Defining The Small Things
Words Definitions
Complex Sentences Complex sentences combine two independent, non-complex sentences with a conjunction.
Note: There are 4 types of sentences. They are: declarative, imperative, interrogative, exclamatory.
  1. • Declarative: a sentence that makes a statement. Uses period.
  2. • Imperative: a sentences that issues a command. Uses period or exclamation mark.
  3. • Interrogative: a sentence that asks a question. Uses question mark.
  4. • Exclamatory: a sentence that projects strong feelings. Uses exclamation mark.
Vocabulary The words used in a language. Formal writing mandates complex and varied language.
Point of View The postition from which something is told.
Note: There are several postitions to write from, but formal writing uses third person point of view which is when the narrator exists outside of the story and calls the characters by name or pronoun. Examples include: he/him, she/her, they/them, etc.
Contraction When two or more words are combined and shorthened using an apostrophe.
Conjunction A word used to connect sentences or clauses together. Some examples include: but, and, because, although, since, yet, etc.
Note: There are 4 types of conjuntions; coordinating, correlative, subordinating, and conjunctive adverbs.
  1. • Coordinating Conjunction: a word that connects two sentences or clauses of equal importance. Examples of this conjunction are: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, and so.
  2. • Correlative Conjunction: a pair of words that join sentences or clauses. Examples of these conjunctions are both...and, either...or, neither...nor, etc.
  3. • Subordinating Conjunction: a conjunction that joins a dominate and subordinate sentence or clause. Examples of subordinating conjunctions are although, because, since, etc.
  4. • Conjunctive Adverb: a cojunction that connects two independent sentences or clauses together. Examples include: nevertheless, thus, hence, etc.
Punctuation
Punctuation When it is used
Period(.) To end sentences.
Comma(,) To separate independent clauses.
Colon(:) To give emphasis, introduce lists or text, and clarify compostition.
Semicolon(;) To join two related but independent clauses in place of a comma and coordinating conjunction.
Ellipsis(...) As a placeholder for omitted text.
Quotation Marks("") To signal when a text is using dialogue from another source or when a character is speaking.
Dash(-) To separate groups of words.
Note: A dash amd hyphen have different uses. There are also two types of dashes, em and en dashes. Hyphens separate words. Em dashes are used to brek up sentences. En dashes are used to show time.
Slash(/) As a alternative to the word "or".
Asterisk(*) To call attention to a footnote.
Parentheses[()] To hold additional information.
Note: Brackets([], {}, or <>) are used to add explanations, clarifications, or corrections.
Ampersand(&) To indicate the word "and" in a listed item.
Question Mark(?) Used to indicate a question.
Exclaimation Point(!) To indicate emotion.
